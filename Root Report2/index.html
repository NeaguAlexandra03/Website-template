What is EUDR?

EUDR stands for the European Union Deforestation Regulation, which aims to minimize the EU's contribution to deforestation and forest degradation globally. It imposes due diligence obligations on companies to ensure that products placed on the EU market are not linked to deforestation or forest degradation. By complying with these rules, companies help protect forests and promote sustainable land use practices.

Additional Information

Copernicus (2014 to the present)

Copernicus, the EU’s flagship Earth Observation Program, was launched in 2014 and has since become a global leader in providing real-time environmental data. Originally conceived as GMES in 1998, it was rebranded upon becoming operational. Offering free and open access to its data, Copernicus is an essential tool for businesses, governments, and NGOs focused on sustainability and compliance. Over the past decade, its expanded satellite network has played a vital role in monitoring ecosystems and addressing global issues like deforestation, climate change, and biodiversity loss, advancing environmental protection and sustainability efforts.

Sentinel-1 ( launched in April 2014)

Sentinel-1 is a key component of the European Copernicus program, launched in April 2014, providing crucial radar imaging capabilities. This satellite constellation, consisting of Sentinel-1A and Sentinel-1B, uses synthetic aperture radar (SAR) to deliver all-weather, day-and-night observations of Earth's surface. Unlike optical satellites, Sentinel-1 can penetrate clouds and operate in any lighting conditions, making it essential for monitoring land cover changes, detecting deforestation, managing natural disasters, and supporting emergency response efforts. Its ability to capture high-resolution images with consistent accuracy plays a vital role in environmental monitoring, infrastructure management, and humanitarian aid.

Sentinel-2 ( launched in June 2015)

Sentinel-2, part of the European Copernicus program, is a pivotal satellite mission launched in June 2015. Equipped with high-resolution optical imaging sensors, Sentinel-2 provides detailed and frequent images of Earth's surface, capturing data in multiple spectral bands. This allows for precise monitoring of land cover, vegetation health, and environmental changes. Sentinel-2's ability to deliver clear, multi-spectral imagery every five days significantly enhances capabilities in agricultural monitoring, deforestation tracking, and disaster management. Its comprehensive data supports sustainable land use planning and contributes to global efforts in climate change mitigation and environmental protection.

Deforestation

Deforestation is the large-scale clearing of forests for purposes such as agriculture, logging, or urban expansion, resulting in severe environmental consequences. It leads to the loss of biodiversity by destroying habitats, contributes to climate change by releasing stored carbon dioxide, increases soil erosion by removing tree roots that stabilize the soil, and disrupts local water cycles by altering precipitation patterns. Additionally, it impacts indigenous communities who depend on forests for their livelihoods and culture. Addressing deforestation involves enforcing regulations, adopting sustainable land management practices, and supporting reforestation efforts to mitigate these effects and preserve vital ecosystems.

NDVI

Your script calculates the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) using Sentinel-2 satellite imagery, which helps in assessing vegetation health. It processes the reflectance values from the red and near-infrared bands of the satellite data to create an NDVI map. This map is then visualized using a color ramp that ranges from dark colors for lower NDVI values (indicating water or barren land) to vibrant greens for higher NDVI values (indicating dense vegetation). The output includes both the colorized NDVI and a data mask to handle areas with missing or unreliable data.
